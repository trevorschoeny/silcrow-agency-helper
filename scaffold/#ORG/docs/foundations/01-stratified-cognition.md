# 01 — Stratified cognition

## What the idea is

Organizations, at every scale and across every culture that has been studied, settle into a small number of distinct *strata* of work — layers that differ not in authority but in the **kind of cognitive work** each layer does. The key observable that distinguishes strata from one another is the *time horizon* of the longest task a role is responsible for. Frontline work resolves itself in days to months. Direct supervision coordinates months of work at a time. Functional management plans in one-to-two-year arcs. Executive work operates in multi-year horizons. At each step, the *kind* of cognition required changes qualitatively.

This is an empirical claim, not a preference. It was developed over roughly sixty years by Elliott Jaques (1917–2003), a Canadian psychoanalyst and organizational theorist who became the most consequential 20th-century thinker on organizational structure. His research — through the Glacier Metal Company, the British Army, the Church of England, the US Army, and corporate clients — accumulated thousands of observations supporting what he eventually called **Stratified Systems Theory**.

Jaques' memorable summary: the work at different strata is "as different in nature as water is different from steam."

## The intellectual history

Jaques began his research in the late 1940s at Glacier Metal Company, a British manufacturing firm. Working with Wilfred Brown, he conducted the **Glacier Project** — a multi-decade engagement that sought to understand how the company actually worked versus how its org chart said it worked. The results were published in *Glacier Project Papers* (Wilfred Brown and Elliott Jaques, Heinemann, 1965) and shaped Jaques' thinking for the next forty years.

Jaques' early insight was the **time span of discretion**. If you ask "how long until the consequences of this person's judgment calls become visible?" — you get radically different answers depending on the role. A machinist might get feedback within hours. A department head might not know for six months whether their planning call was right. A CEO might make decisions whose consequences take five years to play out. Jaques proposed that this time horizon was not incidental — it was the deep variable that explained managerial structure.

Over the next three decades, through *A General Theory of Bureaucracy* (1976) and *Requisite Organization* (1989, 2nd ed. 1996), he built this observation into a full theory. The key works:

- **Jaques, E. (1956).** *Measurement of Responsibility*. Tavistock. The first systematic study of time span.
- **Brown, W. & Jaques, E. (1965).** *Glacier Project Papers*. Heinemann. The foundational empirical base.
- **Jaques, E. (1976).** *A General Theory of Bureaucracy*. Heinemann. Formal theoretical development.
- **Jaques, E. (1990).** "In Praise of Hierarchy." *Harvard Business Review*, January–February 1990. A short, readable defense of stratification against the then-fashionable flat-org enthusiasm.
- **Jaques, E. (1996).** *Requisite Organization* (2nd ed.). Cason Hall. The mature, operational version of the theory.

Jaques was not without critics. Some argued that time-span measurement was harder in practice than he claimed. Others found the idea that organizational levels are *natural* ideologically uncomfortable. A fair reading of the critical literature (see Kleiner's summary in *Strategy + Business* and the materials at globalro.org) concludes that the *pattern* of stratification is robust even where the *precise measurement* is contested.

## The strata

Jaques identified eight strata, each with a characteristic time horizon for the longest task the role is accountable for. (Strata IX and above exist for rare civilization-shaping roles, but are less operationally relevant.)

| Stratum | Time horizon | Character of work | Typical role |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | 1 day – 3 months | Direct task execution; concrete outputs | Individual contributor |
| II | 3 months – 1 year | Coordination of tasks; first-line supervision | Team lead, supervisor |
| III | 1 – 2 years | Functional management; optimizing a workflow | Department head |
| IV | 2 – 5 years | Multi-function integration; general management | Executive / VP |
| V | 5 – 10 years | Business unit strategy | Small-company CEO, large-company EVP |
| VI | 10 – 20 years | Corporate group strategy | CEO of a corporate group |
| VII | 20 – 50 years | Super-corporate strategy | Founder, statesman |
| VIII | 50+ years | Civilization-scale thinking | Nation-builder, founder of durable institutions |

Two important properties:

**The quintave (fractal) property.** Jaques observed that cognitive modes repeat at higher levels with longer horizons and broader scope. A Stratum I worker's relationship to their task mirrors a Stratum V executive's relationship to their business unit. The *pattern* of cognition is fractal; only the *scale* changes. This is why hierarchy feels natural — it reflects how cognition actually stratifies, not arbitrary authority relationships.

**The one-stratum rule.** Each boss should sit *exactly* one stratum above their reports.

- More than one stratum up, and the gap is too wide: the boss can't provide useful cognitive scaffolding, the report feels abandoned, and the boss is under-using their own capacity on work beneath their stratum.
- Less than one stratum up (same stratum), and the boss is doing the same kind of work as the report — which means they are either micromanaging or doing the report's work themselves.

This rule is the single most consequential operational consequence of Jaques' theory. It explains why "flat" organizations past a certain size tend to develop informal hierarchy or collapse under coordination failure, and why top-heavy organizations waste their senior leaders' time.

## Why this matters for the scaffold

The scaffold's four starter roles are placed deliberately:

- **{user_role}** — tier 0, strategic (Stratum III–V, depending on project scope).
- **{lead_role}** — tier 1, architectural (Stratum II–III).
- **{implementer_role}** — tier 2, execution (Stratum I–II).
- **Registrar** — outside the tier structure (not stratified in Jaques' sense).

This gives us the minimum viable stratification. Each tier is exactly one stratum above the next. The spacing is deliberate: close enough for scaffolding, far enough to avoid micromanagement.

The scaffold's operational rules all flow from this:

- **The {lead_role} writes briefs, not specs.** If the {lead_role} writes specs, they are doing {implementer_role} work — collapsing the stratification. The brief/plan exchange preserves the stratum gap.
- **{user_role} does not give direct feedback to the {implementer_role}.** Two-stratum skips are stratification violations.
- **New tiers are added between existing ones only when the work genuinely spans more than one stratum.** Adding tiers for status or political reasons is the most common anti-pattern and destroys the system.

When the scaffold grows past its minimum size, the question is always *whether the work has stratified further*, not "do we need more layers?" If the {lead_role}'s work has bifurcated into (a) genuinely strategic architecture spanning years and (b) tactical coordination across multiple {implementer_role}s on monthly horizons, you have a Stratum III *and* a Stratum II role bundled in one, and splitting them is requisite. If it is just feeling heavy, more process won't help; the problem is elsewhere.

## Debates and open questions

- **Measurement precision.** Jaques and his followers claimed time span can be measured objectively. Critics argue measurement is harder in knowledge work than in 1950s manufacturing. The scaffold takes the pragmatic stance: you don't need to measure time span to the week; you need to recognize qualitative differences in time horizon between tiers.
- **Psychological vs. structural framing.** Jaques often framed strata in terms of individual cognitive capacity — the idea that specific people are "fit" for specific strata. This carries uncomfortable implications. A purely structural reading ("the work at stratum X requires capacity X, whoever does it") avoids them without losing the operational content.
- **Knowledge work vs. operational work.** Some have argued Jaques' model fits manufacturing better than software. The counter-evidence from knowledge-intensive organizations (consultancies, research labs) is strong: stratification exists there too; the strata are just less tied to physical artifacts.
